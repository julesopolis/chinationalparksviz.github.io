our data story


Although Yellowstone is perhaps the National Park that captures people's imaginations most frequently, it is by far not the largest in area nor the most popular in terms of attendance. This graph can be read in the following ways:

    Size of the node indicates the size of the park - the bigger the circle, the larger the park
    The shade of the node indicates its "popularity" in terms of attendance. The darker the node, the more people who freqeunt the parks.
    Other information included in the pop ups are the name of the park, location of the park, date established, and whether it is a World Heritage Site.

Visualizing the parks in this way demonstrates the variation in size, popularity, and may help to change perceptions of National Parks that people may have held.

You may find these insightful, wise analyses helpful as you struggle to comprehend the glory of this visualization:

    You will notice that a relatively small park, Great Smoky Mountains National Park, actually has the most visitors each year. This is likely due at least in part to its central location relative to large population centers in the Eastern U.S.
    Some of the largest parks have the lowest attendance, because they're in the fucking Artic Circle.
    Many national parks have bears. It is unclear at this time how that affects popularity of the parks, but we believe more bears=more people=more dead people.

Overall, the visualization seems to indicate an inverse relationship between number of visitors and size of park/difficulty of access.
